My parents crossed an ocean. They came from Nepal — a country of Himalayan peaks, ancient trade routes, and a culture that treats education as the most sacred form of inheritance — and settled in Concord, North Carolina, where I was born and raised.

Growing up Nepali-American gave me something I didn't fully understand until recently: the ability to see systems from outside themselves. I learned early that the rules everyone around me took for granted were not universal. That discomfort became intellectual curiosity.

"I use data to understand decisions. I build things with what I learn."

Academically, I'm ranked first in my class of 444 at Cox Mill High School with a 4.0 unweighted GPA. These numbers matter less to me than what they represent: sustained intellectual seriousness alongside varsity athletics and founding a nonprofit from nothing.

My intellectual home is behavioral economics and statistics. I've been self-studying university-level economics through the CORE Econ curriculum and am preparing for the USAEO and Economics Olympiad Americas. Last summer I conducted research at the USC Darla Moore Business School on the Chief AI Officer role in corporate strategy.

I also founded Men's Mill Mind, a mental health nonprofit for young men, and serve as Director of Hospitality at DECA. I play varsity soccer having spent years in the MLS Next Academy. I don't separate these parts of myself — they're the same person.

Chapter Two · Destination

UNC Chapel Hill
& Morehead-Cain

UNC Chapel Hill is not a default choice. It is a deliberate one. I intend to study Statistics with an Economics minor — a combination that maps precisely onto who I am becoming: someone who quantifies human behavior and builds models that explain it.

"Chapel Hill is where I believe I can do the most meaningful early work of my life."

The Morehead-Cain Scholarship is not simply a financial instrument. It is a community organized around a specific set of values: leadership in service of something larger than personal advancement, intellectual seriousness combined with practical action. I have been practicing both my whole life.

The scholarship's four summers of leadership development, its network, and its resources for research and civic work align exactly with where my life is already moving. I am not asking to be shaped. I am asking to be equipped.

Soccer has been in my life as long as economics. Playing at the MLS Next Academy level was not just athletic — it was a seminar in systems thinking: how individual decisions compound into collective outcomes, how pressure reveals character, how preparation creates the illusion of talent.

"The pitch taught me that intelligence without execution is just theory."

I earned All-Conference honors at Cox Mill, where I play varsity. I'm now turning that background into a data-driven soccer training business — the intersection of my two identities as economist and athlete, built into a single venture. The discipline soccer demands translates to every other part of my life. I don't separate my athletic self from my academic self. They're the same person.
